1. Executive Summary

1,400 experts were identified as "Outliers" based on elevated CST (Customer Service Time) rankings. A controlled coaching experiment was conducted across 8 groups (coached between 3/25 – 4/1/2026) against a stable non-coached control group of 391 experts. This report evaluates the effectiveness of coaching in reducing CST.

Key Finding: 7 out of 8 coached groups showed CST improvement, with Group 8 achieving the largest improvement (−0.72) and Group 4 close behind (−0.60). Group 1 was the only coached group where CST increased, driven by a shift in SKU mix toward higher-complexity Premium returns. Notably, the non-coached control group also showed natural CST improvement in most periods — and in some cases matched or exceeded coached groups — raising questions about the incremental efficiency of personalized coaching.

2. Methodology

  • Control group: 391 non-coached experts (same group used across all comparisons)
  • Pre-period: 7-day window before coaching date (engagement date within window)
  • Post-period: 7-day window starting on coaching date (engagement date within window)
  • Completion rule: Engagements must be completed within 7 days of the engagement start date
  • Metric: CST (Customer Service Time) — lower is better
  • SKU breakdown: Basic, Deluxe, Premium (increasing complexity & typical CST)

9. Conclusions & Recommendations

Key Findings

  1. Coaching works, but with variable effectiveness. 7 of 8 coached groups showed CST improvement post-coaching. Groups 4 and 8 achieved the largest reductions (−0.60 and −0.72 respectively).
  2. Group 8 was the standout with −0.72 improvement — notably while the control group actually worsened (+0.08). Improvement was strong across all three SKU tiers (Basic −0.68, Deluxe −0.35, Premium −0.62).
  3. SKU mix is a confounding factor. Group 1's apparent CST increase was driven by a ~10% shift toward Premium returns, not actual performance degradation. Adjusted for constant SKU mix, Group 1 would have shown −0.12 improvement.
  4. Later groups (5 & 6) showed diminished returns. The hypothesis is that experts in these groups had already become aware of the CST initiative and began self-correcting on Basic returns before being coached, reducing the marginal impact of coaching.
  5. Basic CST responds well to coaching (improved in 7/8 groups). Deluxe and Premium are more inconsistent, suggesting these require more specialized coaching content.
  6. Coaching efficiency question. The non-coached control group also showed natural CST improvement in most time windows. In some cases the control group improved as much or more than coached groups (e.g., Groups 1, 5, 6), raising questions about the incremental value of personalized coaching for certain populations.
  7. Control group improvement is a diminishing trend. The control group (same 391 experts with the evaluation timeframe shifting daily) initially showed strong improvement (−0.12 for G1 window, −0.16 for G2, −0.24 for G3, −0.27 for G4), but each subsequent timeframe showed progressively less improvement (−0.22, −0.14, −0.03), eventually resulting in worse CST for the Group 8 timeframe (+0.08). This suggests the natural CST improvement observed early on was not sustainable and was likely driven by the specific time windows rather than lasting behavioral change.
  8. Coaching is most effective for mid-to-high proficiency experts. PL3 experts showed the strongest coaching response (−0.39 CST change), followed by PL4 (−0.17). PL2 was essentially flat (+0.02), and PL1 data was too sparse for conclusions (7 experts). This suggests experienced experts are better equipped to translate coaching into measurable performance gains.
  9. Experts moved closer to their Weighted Peer Goal after coaching. Average PeerGoalVar decreased from +0.86 (Pre) to +0.77 (Post), and the share of experts beating their personalized goal rose from 34.4% to 36.7%. Group 8 led with a +22.1 pp increase in beat-goal rate (35.0% → 57.1%), while Group 5 showed the largest variance improvement (−0.39). Groups 1 and 2 were the only groups where the beat-goal rate declined post-coaching.

Recommendations

  1. Continue coaching with SKU-specific content. Develop targeted coaching materials for Deluxe and Premium return types where improvement has been inconsistent.
  2. Account for SKU mix in evaluation. Use SKU-adjusted CST (holding pre-coaching SKU distribution constant) as a supplementary metric to avoid misleading conclusions.
